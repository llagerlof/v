CUBE

- A short science fiction story -

The cube stood alone on the rim of a nameless crater, where no impact should have deposited it and no human mission had ever landed.

Commander Elena Ortiz spotted the sharp reflection first. At a distance, it looked like a trick of sunlight on fractured basalt. Through her visor, however, the object resolved into something impossible: a perfect metal cube, precisely one meter on each side, half-buried in lunar dust that had remained undisturbed for millions of years.

"Either someone beat us here," she said, "or physics has developed a sense of humor."

The three astronauts approached cautiously. Along with Ortiz were systems engineer David Chen and geologist Anika Rao. Every measurement deepened the puzzle. The alloy resisted laser spectroscopy. It reflected nearly every wavelength yet emitted almost no thermal radiation. It possessed no seams, markings, or signs of manufacture.

The dust surrounding it, however, told a different story.

Rao knelt beside the cube. "The regolith layers are intact. This object has been here longer than this crater."

Chen frowned. "That would make it older than the crater itself."

"Which is impossible."

"Obviously."

Science often begins with obvious impossibilities.

Back inside their rover, they compared observations. If the cube had rested there before the impact, it should have been destroyed. If it had arrived afterward, it should have disturbed the dust. Neither had happened.

The mystery remained until Rao requested a gravity scan rather than another surface analysis.

The results silenced the cabin.

The cube extended downward.

Not physically. Gravitationally.

Its mass distribution continued beneath the surface as though the visible cube were merely the exposed corner of a much larger object buried kilometers deep.

"That's impossible," Chen whispered.

Ortiz smiled faintly.

"So was the cube."

Further measurements revealed an astonishing pattern. Tiny variations in the Moon's local gravitational field aligned with ancient lava tubes scattered beneath the region. The buried structure was not random. It formed a network.

Rao suddenly saw what everyone else had missed.

"It isn't a building," she said. "It's a map."

The cube's orientation matched the tunnels precisely. Every edge pointed toward another gravitational anomaly.

Not roads.

Coordinates.

The realization changed everything. The visible cube was not the artifact. It was the label.

Chen compared the network against astronomical simulations. After accounting for the slow drift of the Solar System through the galaxy over billions of years, the directions converged, not on stars as they exist today, but on stars as they had existed nearly four billion years ago.

Most were now dead.

The civilization that built the network had not been navigating space.

They had been preserving memory.

Before humanity possessed even single-celled ancestors, an unknown species had crossed the young Solar System. Knowing their own extinction was inevitable, they left behind durable monuments on airless worlds, places where erosion would not erase them. The Moon became an archive. The cube was its index.

Not a beacon.

Not a weapon.

A library catalog whose books lay hidden beneath the lunar crust.

Ortiz looked once more through the rover window. The cube remained perfectly still in the harsh sunlight, indifferent to the species that had finally learned to ask the correct question.

Humanity had arrived expecting to discover evidence that it was not alone.

Instead, it had discovered something more profound.

It was not the first civilization to wonder whether knowledge could outlive its creators.

As the three astronauts marked the site for future expeditions, they left the cube exactly where they had found it.

The Moon, silent for billions of years, had surrendered only the first page of a very old story.
